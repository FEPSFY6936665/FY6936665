Introduction

    Understanding a galaxy’s evolution and the type of galaxies that planets exist in matters greatly in interpreting the
    required conditions for life supporting environments. Galaxies are often described as grand scale systems of gas, dust,
    and gravity where these elements interact to create stars and enrich space with heavy elements upon their death.
    This influences a galaxy’s capability to host potential planetary systems filled with life. This article aims to study
    and compare two types of major galaxies, elliptical and spiral. These two types of galaxies display different evolutionary
    histories and star formation behaviours, which will be the primary focus regarding the galaxies. These differences are
    thought to shape the environments in which stars, and life supporting systems such as planets, may form. Firstly, the
    article describes the basis of star formation, elliptical and spiral galaxies followed by the processes and the varying
    rates of star formation in each galaxy. Finally, the impact these differences have on habitability, respectively, is considered.

Stars and their Formation

    As outlined above, an initial overview of stars and their formations will be described, and this is important as it will make
    the following parts of this article easier to understand.

    Stars are made up of primarily hydrogen alongside helium and other heavier elements, bound by gravity to form extremely hot
    spheres of gas with their life cycles, determined by their mass, ranging between a few million to trillions of years (NASA, 2025b).

    The formation/birth of a star is thought to occur (NASA, 2025b) within cold molecular clouds (large regions of dense
    concentrations of cold gas and dust that can span hundreds of light years) where local concentrations of gas and dust accumulate
    mass until these dense clumps collapse under their own increasing gravitational strength. As this contraction due to gravity
    continues (McKee and Ostriker, 2007), the temperature of the collapsing material increases rapidly under friction and compression
    until eventually a protostar is developed. A protostar is a “baby star” (NASA, 2025b, para. 3).

    The gravitational collapse of the protostar continues (NASA, 2025b, para. 4) and when the appropriate conditions of extreme
    temperature and pressure are achieved inside the protostar’s core, nuclear fusion ignites (McKee and Ostriker, 2007). This results
    in a release of energy that stabilises the star from further gravitational collapse. According to NASA (2025b, para. 5) these stars
    are “stably undergoing nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium” and are called “main sequence stars”, which is typically what stars
    are pictured as.

    Furthermore, the rate of star formation depends strongly on the availability of cold gas and dust within a galaxy (Kennicutt, 1998)
    indicating star formation is that galaxies that have an abundance of cold gas and dust are more likely to sustain higher rates of active
    star formations/form stars actively compared to galaxies with lesser amounts of cold gas and dust.

    This is essential in understanding why the distinctions in elliptical and spiral galaxies result in contrasting star populations and
    evolutionary pathways, as will be discussed in the following sections.

Elliptical and Spiral galaxies?

    Galaxies are a collection of stars, planets, gas, and dust that are held
    together by gravity with a supermassive black hole at the centre.
    Elliptical galaxies are either round or oval and contain little amounts of cold
    gas and dust.
    
    On the other hand, disc galaxies are flat rotating discs that contain rich
    amounts of gas and dust. Spiral galaxies are a type of disc galaxy, named as
    such due to their spiral arms emerging from the centre of the galaxy. In this
    article, spiral galaxies will be the basis of discussion regarding disc galaxies.
    These galaxies usually have an abundance of cold gas and dust.
