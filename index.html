Introduction

  

    The main discussion of life in the universe is often of planets and whether their
    conditions are suitable for life. However, the type of galaxies that the planets reside
    matters greatly too. Galaxies can be thought of as factories that create stars and
    through the whole cycle of these star’s formation, life and death, enrich space with
    heavy elements and create the conditions for potential planetary life. Today, the
    observation of galaxies has advanced our understanding of cosmic history and
    evolution of the universe including the emergence of life in our own Milky Way. This
    article aims to compare two types of major galaxies, elliptical and disc (spiral)
    galaxies, and investigate the differences as well as the evolutions of their star
    formation behaviours. The article will also explore how their differences can
    influence the formation of life supporting environments in the areas around them.
    Firstly, the basics of star formations, elliptical and disc galaxies are described
    followed by the history and processes of star formations in each galaxy. Finally, the
    impact that each type of galaxy and their differing star formations have on
    habitability is discussed.

What are elliptical and disc galaxies?

  

  
    Galaxies are a collection of stars, planets, gas, and dust that are held
    together by gravity with a supermassive black hole at the centre.
    Elliptical galaxies are either round or oval and contain little amounts of cold
    gas and dust.
    
    On the other hand, disc galaxies are flat rotating discs that contain rich
    amounts of gas and dust. Spiral galaxies are a type of disc galaxy, named as
    such due to their spiral arms emerging from the centre of the galaxy. In this
    article, spiral galaxies will be the basis of discussion regarding disc galaxies.
    These galaxies usually have an abundance of cold gas and dust.
