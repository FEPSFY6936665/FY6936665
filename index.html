Abstract

Introduction

    Understanding a galaxy’s evolution and the type of galaxies that exist is important in interpreting the determinants for star formation.
    Galaxies are often described as grand-scale systems of gas, dust, and gravity where these elements interact to create stars (NASA, 2025a).
    Several factors can influence a galaxy’s ability for star formation, including gas availability (McKee and Ostriker, 2007), which will be
    the focus. This article aims to study and compare two types of galaxies with different evolutionary histories and star formation behaviours,
    elliptical and spiral galaxies. It will also explore how their differing gas contents influence star formation rates, respectively. Firstly,
    the article explains the basis of star formations, followed by descriptions of elliptical and spiral galaxies. Finally, the processes and
    the varying rates of star formation in each galaxy are discussed.

Analysis and discussion

Stars and their Formation

    Stars are composed of primarily gases of hydrogen alongside helium and other heavier elements, bound by gravity to form extremely
    hot spheres of gas (NASA, 2025b).

    The formation of a star generally occurs (NASA, 2025b) within molecular clouds (large regions of dense amounts of gas and dust), where local
    concentrations of gas and dust accumulate mass until these dense clumps collapse under their own increasing gravitational strength (McKee and Ostriker, 2007).
    The temperature of the collapsing material increases rapidly under friction and compression until a protostar is formed. A protostar is a ‘baby star’ (NASA, 2025b, para. 3).

    As the collapse continues (NASA, 2025b, para. 4), conditions of extreme temperature and pressure are achieved inside the protostar’s core,
    and nuclear fusion ignites (McKee and Ostriker, 2007). This results in an energy release that stabilises the star from further gravitational
    collapse. According to NASA (2025b, para. 5), these stars are ‘stably undergoing nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium’ and are called
    ‘main sequence stars’, which is typically what stars are pictured as.

    Furthermore, the rate of star formation depends strongly on the availability of gas (hydrogen and helium) within a galaxy (Kennicutt, 1998, p. 224),
    indicating that galaxies with an abundance of gas are more likely to sustain higher rates of star formation compared to galaxies with lesser amounts.

Elliptical and Spiral galaxies

    Galaxies are enormous systems composed of stars, gas, and dust, held together by gravity, with most large galaxies having a supermassive black hole at the
    centre. Common classifications of galaxies are elliptical and spiral galaxies, where both differ in structure and composition (NASA, 2025a).

    According to NASA (2024a, para. 6), elliptical galaxies display round or oval characteristics with little structure
    and are ‘less common than spiral galaxies.’ Elliptical galaxies can also be characterised by their scarcity of gas, holding predominantly older,
    red star populations, and are held as the largest types of galaxies in the local Universe (De Lucia et al., 2006, p. 499).

    Alternatively, spiral galaxies display a flat rotating structure (NASA, 2024a) and are rich in gas. A common characteristic is the spiral arms
    emerging from the centre of the galaxy, where there are high concentrations of gas and stars. This is shown in Figure 1. Furthermore, spiral galaxies are found to have a
    dominant population of younger, blue stars alongside older, red stars (Kennicutt 1998, pp. 208-224; Dobbs and Baba, 2014).

    The result of these differences presents contrasting star populations, which link to star formation rates. In the next section, the star formations of each type of
    galaxy will be discussed.

Star Formations in the Galaxies

    Firstly, research (De Lucia et al., 2006) has found that elliptical galaxies evolved from forming stars in a single intense burst early in their history, to
    having an incredibly low rate of star formation in the present day. Additionally, the lack of gas in the present day supports this research, as without a large
    store of gas, a galaxy will be unable to undergo active star formation. This suggests elliptical galaxies have used up a considerable amount of their gas stores
    and are now incapable of forming new stars actively, and due to having a very small percentage of young stars compared to old stars, scientists have called these
    kinds of galaxies ‘Red and Dead Galaxies’ (NASA, 2023).

    Highly active star formation occurs within spiral galaxies (Dobbs and Baba, 2014), which is typically concentrated inside the spiral arms due to their dense
    amounts of cold gas (McKee and Ostriker, 2007, p. 603), and as such conditions are favourable for stars to form in. This great supply of gas in spiral galaxies
    is the most likely explanation for a spiral galaxy’s ability to form new stars in the present day, leading to a greater population of young stars than in elliptical
    galaxies. Regarding spiral galaxies’ star formation history, early and late types of spiral galaxies are ‘predicted to have comparable SFRs’ (Kennicutt, 1998, p. 223)
    (SFRs is Star Formation Rates). Thus, it could be argued that spiral galaxies have been forming stars at stable rates throughout their history.

Conclusion

    The purpose of this article was to examine the differences of elliptical and spiral galaxies and consider how these have influenced their star
    formation behaviours throughout history. Elliptical galaxies seem to have formed most of their stars early in their history and have now depleted
    their gas contents, whereas spiral galaxies have retained enough of their gas and can sustain active star formations in the present day. Hence, 
    the ages and distribution of the types of stars have been influenced within each galaxy and have allowed astronomers to identify the role that gas
    availability has on the evolution of galaxies.
